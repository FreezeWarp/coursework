  • Maine & Computers in Schools

    Computers as an educational tool are nothing new; nearly a decade ago, in 2003, 90% of students between the ages of 5 and 17 used computers in school (“Table 19”), 48.0% of whom were also able to access the internet (“Table 15”). Back at home students fared even better, with 70.7% having access to a computer (“Table 18”) and 58.0% access to the internet (“Table 15”). Five years later, in 2008, 97% of teachers reported having at least one computer in their classroom, while for every three students one internet-connected computer would be available for use (“Table 109”).

    In spite of this gradual and continuing computerisation of the classroom, many schools remain precariously unconnected and in the dark. A worrying new “separate-but-equal” has arisen in the state of public education that is never debated at a national level, never mentioned alongside preschool education in State of the Union Addresses. In the most recently compiled figures, the National Center for Education Statistics reported that while 80% of white students had access to a computer in their home, only 65% of black students and 71% of Hispanic students were as propitious (“Table 18”). Similarly, while 73% of white students attested to having access to the internet, only 53% of black students said they were as auspicious. Even fewer hispanics reported having access, a mere 49% (“Table 15”).
